Chinese Food Therapy

  • Culture
  • Food

Chinese food therapy or nutrition therapy is an ancient practice in Traditional Chinese medicine that relies mainly on the consumption of natural food to heal the body. In Western countries, physicians generally regard dietary changes as an important part of medical treatment but not the primary cure to most diseases. Traditional Chinese medicine views the nature and functions of the human body in a unique way, even how diseases manifest and how they are treated.

Chinese Medicine believes that illnesses can be treated by eating a certain amount of specific combinations of food instead of taking medicines. Chinese food therapy is based on the well-known fact that improper eating leads to illnesses and proper diet contributes to the body’s health.

There are four food classifications in the Chinese diet: grains, vegetables, fruit/nuts, and meats. The first two are essential to sustain life (30 to 40% of diet) and the last two are supplementary food that should be consumed in moderation (10 to 15% of diet). Chinese people also classify food according to the five tastes, each acting on or influencing a particular vital organ. The five tastes are:

  1. Sweet (acts on spleen/stomach);
  2. Sour (acts on liver/gall bladder);
  3. Bitter (acts on heart/small intestine);
  4. Salty (acts on kidney/bladder);
  5. Pungent(acts on lungs/large intestine).

The proper diet should combine the five tastes in good balance to promote internal body harmony. Practitioners also believe that food has different natural properties and they can be neutral or possess either Yin or Yang properties. Yin foods are cold or cool while Yang foods are hot or warm.

There are specific symptoms indicated in Chinese food therapy to identify a person’s body constitution. For example, a person with cold extremities even in the summertime who tires easily and is overweight by 20 pounds is said to have a cold body type and should eat more of Yang (hot/warm) food to maintain the body’s balance.

Eating food according to the body’s constitution is fundamental to stay healthy. It is important to understand the nature of food and its critical role in preventing and treating ailments and diseases. Even with the existence of very high-tech medical technologies today, keep in mind that food is still the most important pillar of health.

Cucumbers and Tomatoes

Speaking of cucumber, many people like to eat it, but few people know its pharmaceutical value. Traditional Chinese medicine believes that the cucumber is “cool” in nature. It has the effect of detoxification and could help to ease throat pain caused by some illnesses. Modem medical researchers find that cucumber is rich in vitamin E, which helps to fight against aging. Women like to put cucumber juice or film on face to smooth skin and reduce wrinkles. Cucumber also contains vitamin B 1, which is essential to the brain and nerve system. Tomatoes are “cool” in nature and known to be a powerhouse of nutrition. Tomatoes have the effectiveness of detoxification, reducing blood pressure, preventing from developing cancer and aging. It contains a multitude of vitamins and minerals that can promote the immune system and support health. In addition, a tomato is high in nutrition, but low in calorie so it is an excellent choice for a weight-loss diet

Two Suggestions from TCM Doctors

When TCM doctors talk about disease,they speak of internal imbalances that cause one’s “Qi” (vital energy) or blood to stagnate. The following are two key suggestions to maintain a healthy body

  • No. 1 Eat a hearty breakfast and a light dinner

“Qi” moves through the body cyclically, so different organs are more energized at different points of the day. The stomach and spleen function optimally in the morning, between 7 am and 11 am You should avoid eating too much outside of this window. In particular, indulging in late-night snacks stresses your digestive system, leading to indigestion and weight gain.

  • No. 2 Avoid cold and raw foods.

According to TCM, the body transforms food by heating it with “digestive fire”. That’s why cooked food is easy to digest,whereas cold and raw foods (like salads and sushi) take up extra energy, causing the digestive process to work less well. To avoid taxing the stomach, eat food that’s been properly cooked
