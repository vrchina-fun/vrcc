Chinese Zodiac Stories

  • Folklore
  • Festival , Legend , Personage

There are many legends and mythology related to the Chinese zodiac and there are various versions of stories popular in different regions. Why were there twelve animals in the zodiac calendar and how did the scheme order come from? Here are the most well-known stories.

Order of the Animals

This is the most widespread legend about the Chinese zodiac. The Jade Emperor who was the Emperor in Heaven in Chinese folklore ordered that animals would be designated as calendar signs and the twelve ones that arrive first would be selected. At that time, the cat and the mice were good friends and neighbors. When they heard of this news, the cat said to the mouse: “We should arrive early to sign up, but I usually get up late.” The mouse then promised to awaken his friend and go together. However, in the morning when he got up, he was too excited to recall his promise and went directly to the gathering place. On the way, he encountered the tiger, ox, horse and other animals that ran much faster.

In order not to fall behind them, he thought up a good idea. He made the straightforward ox carry him on condition that he would sing for the ox. At last, he and the ox arrived first The ox was happy thinking that he would be the first sign of the year, but the mouse had already slid in front and became the first lucky animal of the Chinese zodiac. Meanwhile, his neighbor, the cat, was too late so when it finally arrived, the selection was over. That’s why other animals appear behind the little mouse and why the cat hates the mouse so much that every time they meet, the cat will chase and kill it.

There are various versions of this story. Some say it was the Yellow Emperor who intended to select twelve guards. It was also said that the cat hoped that the mouse could get there early and sign up on behalf, but the mouse completely forgot it or maybe just did that intentionally. Some even said that the animals were requested to have a swimming race. The elephant was said to participate in the race too but ran away finally because the mouse got into its trunk.

Number of Zodiac Animals’ Toes

Many famous scholars in history had their interpretations of this interesting topic. A scholar in the Song Dynasty explained his thought based on Yin Yang Theory. Among the twelve animals: mouse, tiger, dragon, monkey, and dog have five toes. Five is an odd number which is thought to be in yang side or positive power. The horse has one toe, also an odd number. Other animals have toes of an even number which is thought to be yin, negative power. A snake has no toe but its tongue has two tips, for this reason, a snake is put in the even number group. Yin and yang animal signs were interlaced<1>. Some scholars of the Ming Dynasty followed and developed this idea.

Chang’e and Jade Rabbit

Moon Palace, also called Guanghan Palace, refers to the Palace on the moon_ Legend goes that it is inhabited by the goddess of the moon- Chang’e, the Jade Rabbit and the woodcutter Wu Gang_ As is known to all, Chang’ e took the pill of immortality and flew to the moon and then she became the Chinese goddess of the moon. There were many versions about the background of the goddess’ best friend- the Jade Rabbit It was widely believed that it was sent to the Palace to alleviate sorrowful solitude of Chang’ e_ It was busy all day long pestling medicinal materials in efforts to concoct a pill that could help the goddess return to Earth_ However, it never succeeded.

Ox-Head and Horse-Face

Ox-head and Horse-face are two guardians or types of guardians of the Underworld in Chinese mythology_ As indicated by their names, they both have the bodies of men, but Ox-Head has the head of an ox while Horse-Face has the face of a horse. They are the first beings a dead soul encounters upon entering the Underworld; in many stories, they directly escort the newly dead to the Underworld_ The origin of the story is a bit difficult to find_ In the Chinese classical novel Journey to the West, Ox-Head and Horse-Face are sent to capture Sun Wukong, but he overpowers them and scares them away_ In Japanese mythology, Ox-Head and Horse-Face are known as “Gozu” and “Mezu” respectively.
