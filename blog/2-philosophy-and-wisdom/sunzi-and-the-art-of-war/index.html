Sunzi and The Art of War

  • Culture
  • Philosophy , Personage , History

Sunzi is the reputed author of “The Art of War”, which is considered the best single book ever written on the subject of war. Of Sunzi himself, little is known beyond his birth in the state of Qi and service to the emperor Helti of Wu. He was a military specialist active during the turbulent Spring and Autumn period. Recent excavations of ancient manuscripts of Sunzi have confirmed the great antiquity of the text attributed to him. Long studied in Asia, Sunzi’s work became known in the West only in the late eighteenth century and was not properly translated until the twentieth.

Sunzi’s approach to warfare, unlike that of Western authors, does not put the force at the center: indeed, the Chinese character Li, which means force, occurs only nine times in the text’s thirteen chapters. This reflects the conditions of warfare in China at the time as well as Sunzi ’s conviction that victory and defeat are fundamentally psychological states. He sees war, therefore, not so much as a matter of destroying the enemy materially and physically, although that may play a role, of unsettling the enemy psychologically; his goal is to force the enemy’s leadership and society from a condition of harmony, in which they can resist effectively, toward one of chaos, which is tantamount to defeat.

Military action is presented by Sunzi in an implicitly Daoist frame of reference. The idea that terrain, weather and enemy morale in effect have contours, through which the successful general finds the best ways, thereby using the force inherent in them to support his purposes, is Daoist, as is the recurrent theme of transformation, from one state to another, as in the interplay he discusses between regular and irregular forces.

But operationally, Sunzi’s goal is psychological dominance and its exploitation, founded upon superior knowledge of the enemy (he lays great stress on the employment of secret agents) and Kueitao, variously translated as “deception” or “unconventional means”. Thus Sunzi commends operations that will harm enemy morale: splitting alliances, evading battle, attacking by surprise; he condemns those that may undermine one’s society, such as the attrition that might result from besieging a walled city. Some operations may be almost purely psychological in their purpose, such as setting fires, which Sunzi discusses in effect as a terror weapon. The height of military skill is to turn opposing plans to one’s uses by “attacking the enemy’s strategy”, which at its best yields victory without fighting.

Sunzi, however, does not propose that combat can be eliminated. Rather, he is mindful of the tremendous risks attendant on any resort to force, especially bankruptcy and the social disintegration of the politically weak Chinese states of his time. So he urges that force not be squandered, but conserved carefully and used only when it may have a decisive impact.

Sun Bin

Sun Bin was a military strategist born in Qi State. He was considered as one of the most outstanding military strategists and politicians of his time, after his ancestor Sun Tzu. Sun Bin displayed talent from an early age. His cleverness and wit were the cause of envy from a classmate Pang Juan, who later became a strategic general in the State of Wei. When the two boys grew up, Pang deceived Sun into going to the State of Wei and framed him for being a traitor. Sun Bin suffered from corporal punishment and his “Bin” (kneecaps) were removed. Because of this Bin punishment, he was called Sun Bin in ancient history books. Sun Bin amassed a collection of his military strategies, which he named Sun Bin ’s Art of War.

Zhuge Liang

Zhuge Liang was a chancellor of the state of Shu Han during the Three Kingdoms period. He is widely recognized as the most accomplished strategist of his era and has been compared to Sun Tzu. Zhuge Liang emphasized the importance of the army to the country. He thought the directing of military affairs should focus on the army’s discipline and maintaining of required standards for appearance and bearing, training soldiers and fair making in reward or punishment, etc. The directing of military affairs not only depends on the discipline of the army and the power of general but also on the education of the soldiers.
