Beginning of the World

  • Folklore
  • Legend , Personage , Literature

It may seem that every culture has its tale about the creation of the world and the beginning of mankind, so it is true of China.

The beginning of the universe was nothing but chaos, and the heaven and the earth were intermingled - big black egg being commonly used as an analogy. Pangu was born inside this egg and slept for 18,000 years, during which time the Yin and Yang balanced as he grew. When he awoke, he realized he was trapped within it He cracked the egg and began to push it apart, essentially splitting the Yin and Yang. The upper half of the shell became the sky above him and the lower half became the earth. The longer he held them apart, the thicker they grew and the taller he became, thus pushing them further apart - by precisely ten feet per day. Here versions begin to change. Some claim that a turtle, a Qilin, a phoenix, and a dragon assisted him in this task After another 18,000 years Pangu died, his body forming the various parts of the earth and the parasites on his body forming humans. Another version stated that he formed the earth with a chisel and hammer, while yet another version states that a goddess who later inhabited the earth created humans.

According to this myth, Pangu was the first supreme being and the originator of the heaven and the earth. He is typically depicted as a dwarf- though he is actually a giant - covered in hair or bearskin or leaves, with horns fixed atop his head and either a chiseler, a hammer or an egg in his hand. Other tales speak of Pangu as a creature from heaven that had the head of a dog and the body of a man and directly accredit Pangu as the father of mankind, while another version claims he molded men from clay.

The interesting aspects of this tale are its similarities to other myths. For example, the cosmic egg is a common concept that is indicative of the universe before the scientific theory of the Big Bang occurred. While this may, at first glance, be a very primitive way of describing such an event, one cannot help but notice how very insightful it is. How did various people with no apparent technology or knowledge of the universe, as we modem humans know it, so accurately explain what we now can? Were they made privy to this knowledge somehow?

Another interesting aspect of the tale is one of the more elusive. Some versions of the Pangu creation myth state that the giant had help from four mythical beasts. First, the turtle: the Chinese were not the only ones to use it in their creation myth; various world myths, creation and otherwise, include the turtle for its strength and immortality. The Qilin, though indigenous to Asian mythology, is said to have been dragon-like. Of course, dragons are central to Asian mythology- though also found world-wide - as bearers of wisdom and a symbol of power, also connected to the succession of the early emperors. How so many cultures separated by thousands of miles came to describe such similar occurrences has been a subject of much intrigue over the centuries.

The Story of Nǚwa

Nǚwa is a goddess in the ancient Chinese legend known for creating mankind and repairing the sky. It is said that Nǚwa existed at the beginning of the world and created men from yellow clay. But later there was a fight between the god of water and the god of fire, which broke Mount Buzhou. Since Mount Buzhou was a pillar holding up the sky, the sky began to fall. Nǚwa was really concerned about her people so she picked out colorful stones in Mount Tiantai and used them to seal the broken sky. In this way, peace and happiness returned to mankind.

Jingwei Filling the Sea

According to Chinese mythology, Jingwei is the daughter of the emperor Yandi. She perishes at a young age in the East Sea. After her death, she chooses to assume the shape of a bird in order to take revenge upon the sea by bringing stones and small twigs from the mountains nearby over the sea in an effort to fill it up. Jingwei even has a short dialogue with the sea when the sea scoffs her. She claims that she will proceed to take one hundred million years, whatever it takes to fill up the sea so that others would not have to perish as she did.
