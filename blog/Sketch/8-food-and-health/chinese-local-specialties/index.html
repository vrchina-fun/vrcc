Chinese Local Specialties

  • Culture
  • Food

Local specialties in tourist cities of China have become a part of tourism. While enjoying sightseeing, visitors can also have a taste of all kinds of specialties in China. The following are some of the famous local specialties.

Street Snack “Jianbing Guozi”

“Jianbing Guozi” was listed into the fourth batch of the intangible cultural heritage of the Tianjin municipality in 2017. Made of mung bean flour, eggs, chopped green onion, fried breadstick, sweet sauce made from fermented flour and chili powder, “Jianbing Guozi” has a long history and features one of the most renowned snacks with local flavor in Tianjin.lt is the fourth most distinctive food of Tianjin following steamed stuffed bun, fried rice cake, and 18th Street fried dough twists. It has now become more than a portion of local snack food and can be found in the US, the UK, France, and other countries.

Mung Bean Milk

Mung bean milk is a by-product of cellophane noodle production. It is made from mung beans and the procedure of producing is quite similar to that of soy milk. It is in gray-green and tastes a little bit sour and sweet, with a swill-like smell. Mung bean milk is a kind of healthy food, containing lots of Vitamin C, sugar, fiber and protein. For most local people in Beijing, drinking mung bean milk is a kind of enjoyment, but for the people who taste it for the first time, the swill-like smell would make it hard to swallow.

Crossing-the-Bridge Rice Noodles

Rice noodles, a typical Yunnan snack, are prepared with high-quality rice. Thin, long and soft rice noodles are especially delicious. Crossing-the-Bridge Rice Noodles are meticulously prepared with broth, sliced meat, and seasonings.

Crossing-the-Bridge Rice Noodles originated from Mengzi County in south Yunnan, with a history of more than 100 years. Legend has it that a scholar studied hard every day, preparing for the imperial examination. His wife often delivered rice noodles to his study. Finally, the scholar passed the examination and became Number One Scholar. As his wife must cross a bridge when delivering food to him, the scholar named it “Crossing-the-Bridge Rice Noodles” which later became very popular.

Stinky Tofu

Stinky tofu, or strong-smelling preserved tofu, is smelly, with an “ugly” appearance. However, once you try it, you should enjoy its taste. The stinky tofu in Changsha is the most famous type, with crispy skin and a fresh and spicy taste.

Changsha’s stinky tofu enjoys the most fame in China, followed by that of Nanjing and Shaoxing. The production and tastes of the stinky tofu in these three cities are quite different, but they share a common point: smelling bad but tasting nice. Stinky tofu has become a traditional Chinese snack throughout China, and it is easy to find in the most-traveled cities like Beijing, Shanghai, Xi’an, Guilin, and Hangzhou.

Dongpo Pork

The main ingredient of Dongpo Pork is certainly pork. The pork is cut into pieces about two inches square, consisting of half fat and half lean meat. The dish is named after a revered poet, calligrapher and artist in Song Dynasty, Su Dongpo, who was supposed to have invented, or at least inspired this dish. It is made from a slab of pork belly with a lot of fat, but it will be cooked for a couple of hours before being served which results in fat sans much of its greasiness. The meat is so tender that you can quite easily pry it away in small pieces with chopsticks. People only feel oily but not greasy, with the fragrance of wine. It does not taste as fat as it looks like, for the side food like plainly cooked broccoli and ginger help offset the fat.

It takes at least four hours to make Dongpo Pork during which time it is simmered twice, braised, sautéedeo and steamed.

“Quanjude” Roast Duck

“Quanjude” Roast Duck is one of the gourmet dishes enjoying “China’s Time-honored Brands”. The “Quanjude” firm was founded in 1864. It is one of the most famous catering businesses in China.

Crisp skin, tender and non-greasy meat is the distinctive taste of “Quanjude” Roast Duck. The crisp skin instantly melts in the mouth, with no tough things left behind. When the roast duck is taken out from the oven, a Quanjude chef will slice it into pieces in front of the diners. The slicing skills are so excellent that they are considered as a visual treat to everyone present. Quanjude Roast Duck is rich in various nutritious elements like protein, Vitamin B1, Vitamin B2 and minerals thus welcomed by not only Chinese but foreign visitors.

Origin of Hot Pot

The Chinese hot pot has a history of more than 1,000 years. Hot pot seems to have originated in Mongolia where the main ingredient was meat, usually beef, mutton or horse meat. It then spread to southern China during the Tang Dynasty and was further established during the Mongolian Yuan Dynasty. In time, regional variations developed with different ingredients such as seafood, vegetables, and mushrooms. By the Qing Dynasty, the hot pot became popular throughout most of China. Today in many modem homes, particularly in the big cities, the traditional coal-heated steamboat hot pot has been replaced by electric, gas cooker versions. Since hot pot styles vary the ingredients are different from region to region.
