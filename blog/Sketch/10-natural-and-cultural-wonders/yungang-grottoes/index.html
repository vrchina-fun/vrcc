Yungang Grottoes

  • Geography
  • History , Heritage , Religion

Yungang Grottoes represent the excellent achievement of Buddhist sculptural art of ancient China, and it is the largest among the four most famous ancient grotto complexes in China. Yungang Grottoes with 252 caves and 51,000 Buddhist statues are the classical masterpieces of Chinese Buddhist art in the 5th and 6th centuries. They were first carved out of the sandstone cliffs on Wuzhou Mountain in 453AD, during the Northern Wei Dynasty. The main construction spanned for about forty years, and it was roughly completed in 525AD.

The whole grotto complex is magnificent with delicate carvings. All the statues are precious and vivid, representing the development of art, architecture, music, and religion at that time. Yungang Grottoes extend about one kilometer long from east to west and are divided into east, middle and west parts. Caves in the east part are mainly built-in with pagodas. Caves in the middle part usually have front and back rooms, with the main Buddhist statue in the center, while the walls and the ceilings are carved with sculptures. Caves in the west part are medium and small caves and niches.

Caves No.5 and 6 in the middle part of the Yungang Grottoes are the most impressive ones. Inside the 5th cave is a 17-meter tall Buddhist statue, which is the largest one of Yungang Grottoes. Inside the 6th cave, besides large Buddhist statues, precious sculptures are telling the story of Sakyamuni.

The sculptures inside the caves are all magnificent and multi-colored with excellent workmanship. At the other extreme, the 14-meter-high seated Buddha of Sakyamuni outside Grotto No.20 commands respect not only for its size but also for the face of the figure, which is characterized by soft lines and a pair of eyes that radiate intellectual and spiritual vigor. At the time of the interior adornment of the Mogao and Yungang Grottoes, Buddhism was in ascendance in China. The unique beauty of the Yungang Grottoes was recognized almost immediately upon their completion.

The most culturally significant figures in the Yungang Grottoes, after the Buddha figures, are perhaps the statues of the five Chinese emperors. These important works of art are among the few historical relics to survive the Northern Wei Dynasty.

The emergence of the art of the Yungang Grottoes is intimately linked to the Silk Road that connected China to the outside world from the first century BC to the 16th century AD. The Silk Road was of course primarily a trade route, but it was also a communications route in the broadest sense, i.e., ideas traversed from east to west and vice-versa. One such idea that traveled during the era of the Silk Road was Buddhism. Indeed, the Mogao Grottoes lie directly on the original, northern Silk Road route.

The White Horse Temple

The White Horse Temple is located in Luoyang. According to legend, the Eastern Han emperor Ming dreamed of a golden figure flying over his palace with the sun and moon behind its head. The emperor told his ministers about the dream, and they suggested the figure may be the Buddha in India. A delegation was thus sent to India. After three years, the delegation returned with two eminent Indian Buddhist monks. The monks brought with them a white horse carrying a bundle of Buddhist sutras and figures. Next year, the emperor ordered the construction of the White Horse Temple to honor the arrival of Buddhism and the horse. It became China’s first Buddhist temple.

Namaste Dagoba

Namaste Dagoba was completed in 2009, used to enshrine the only relic of Sakyamuni’s finger bone in the world. The height of the dagoba is 148m. This number is chosen because “1” symbolizes that the Buddha is the only awakened one while other human beings are yet to be enlightened. The “4” and “8” denote the 8th day of the 4th lunar month, the birthday of Buddha. The relic of Sakyamuni’s finger bone is enshrined in the underground palace. It is brought up to the first floor on the first day and 15th day of each lunar month plus Saturdays and Sundays for devotees to pay homage.
