Humble Administrator's Garden

  • Geography
  • Heritage

The beautiful waterside City of Suzhou in Jiangsu Province is most famous for its elegant classical gardens. Among these, the Humble Administrator’s Garden is the largest and most renowned. It is considered as one of China’s four most famous gardens, together with the Beijing Summer Palace, Chengde Mountain Resort and Suzhou Lingering Garden. With a total area of 51,950 m², it is the largest garden in Suzhou. And In 1997, Humble Administrator’s Garden (Zhuozheng Yuan), along with other classical gardens of Suzhou has proclaimed a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

During the reign of the Zhengde Emperor in the Ming Dynasty, the site was occupied by Dahong Temple. At that time, a censor named Wang Xianchen, an imperial envoy and poet of the Ming Dynasty, appropriated the temple and converted it into a private villa. In 1510, he retired to his home town of Suzhou under the occasion of his father ’s death. He had experienced a tumultuous official life punctuated by various demotions and promotions, and gave up his last official post as magistrate of Yongjia county in Zhejiang province, and began to do some gardening work there. He thought that was a simple life led by a humble man like him, hence the name of the garden. But the Wang family could not maintain the garden. After the death of Wang Xianchen a few years later, Wang’s son lost the garden to pay gambling debts. In the coming centuries, the garden repeatedly changed hands and was reconstructed many times by different owners. That’s why the garden we see today is far removed from the one enjoyed by Wang Xianchen.

Today’s garden mainly consists of three parts: the Central Garden which is properly called Zhuozheng Yuan, the East Garden once called Dwelling Upon Return to the Countryside, and a West Garden formally called the Supplementary Garden. And each has its unique character.

The whole garden is water-centered, with green hills, delicate pavilions, and lush flowers and trees, full of poetic and pictorial splendor and rich features of the south of the lower reaches of the Yangtze River. The houses in the residential area are of the typical Suzhou style and now used as exhibition rooms. The middle section is the gem of the whole garden. Of its area one-third is covered by water. Around the lake are trees and buildings.

In this well-preserved private garden, one will enjoy the original and ingenious design as well as the calm mind which deeply hidden in your mind. It is also great fun to have a study on the basic factors of Chinese traditional garden including the lakeside rocks, the tower, and mansions, winding path, and the corridors.

Suzhou Museum and L M. Pei

Suzhou Museum, designed by the universally well-known American-Chinese architect Mr. Ieoh Ming Pei, becomes very famous over the world since its opening in 2006. Just beside the Humble Administrator’s Garden, Suzhou Museum has over 40 thousand historical and cultural relics exhibiting to the public, like, jade, ceramics, bronze, stone, ivory, etc. Suzhou Museum is generally built in a traditional Chinese style, with white walls and black tiles, looking harmonious and elegant among the surrounding ancient buildings. It is said to be the last design of I. M. Pei in his career. Therefore, not only does it become a monumental design building in Suzhou, but also a significant construction, merging the traditional Chinese architectural design with the future.

The Summer Palace

The Summer Palace is said to be the best-preserved imperial garden in the world. It was initially built in 1750 by Emperor Qianlong. In 1860 and 1900 it was destroyed by invaders, and in 1912 it was rebuilt. The architectural style of the Summer Palace is to make others believe that it is inartificial. The landscaped gardens, temples and pavilions are designed to achieve harmony with nature and to please the eye. During the hot Beijing summers, the Imperial Family preferred the beautiful gardens and airy pavilions of the Summer Palace to the walled-in Forbidden City. Empress Cixi took up permanent residence here for a while, giving rise to some tales of extravagance and excess.
