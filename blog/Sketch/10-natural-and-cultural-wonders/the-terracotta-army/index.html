The Terracotta Army

  • Geography
  • Heritage , History

The Terracotta Army is considered one of the greatest archaeological sites in the world, and one of the greatest discoveries of the 20th century. It is significant because the hundreds of detailed life-size models represent the army that triumphed over all other Chinese armies in the Warring States Period, and who ushered in the united imperial China era 2,200 years ago.

Qin Shi Huang, or the First Emperor of the Qin Dynasty, ordered the creation of this army of terracotta statues. It is a form of funerary art buried with the emperor and its purpose is to protect the emperor in his afterlife. The Terracotta Army figures’ excavation is regarded as one of the greatest discoveries of the 20th century. In 1974, when some farmers dug well they discovered what is now considered one of the greatest archaeological sites in the world. Before that, it had lain underground for more than 2,000 years.

The first part of the Terracotta Army site discovered was named Vault One. In 1976, two other vaults were uncovered about 20 meters away and were named Vault Two and Vault Three.

The tomb is a treasury for the Chinese people and the whole world. In December 1987, UNESCO selected the Tomb of the First Emperor, including the Terracotta Army Vaults as a World Cultural Heritage Site.

The three vaults in the Terra-Cotta Warrior Museum are arrayed as the buried army in strict accordance with the ancient directives in The Art of War: facing east towards the ancient enemies of Qin State, with Vault One on the right flank, Vault Two on the left flank, and Vault Three a command post at the rear.

The excavated terracotta figures fall into three major categories: infantry, cavalry, and charioteers. The infantry can be further divided into subcategories, including officers of high, middle, and low ranks, light-armed and heavy-armed foot soldiers and standing and kneeling archers. The charioteers can be further divided into chariot drivers and chariot warriors.

The clothing of the terracotta figures is different You can tell the rank of military service of each figure from its dressing. For example:

  • The general wears two layers of robes beneath an armored tunic that protects his chest, back, and shoulders. He wears square-toed shoes, which are lightweight and curve upwards at the front Only one general was found in Vault one and two in Vault two.
  • Armored warriors wear robes covered by a turtleneck, heavily armored capes designed to protect their chests, backs, and shoulders.
  • Cavalrymen wear pillbox hats, neck scarves and light body armor to the front and back Their shoes are soft and round at the toes so as not to injure their mounts.
  • Chariot drivers have extra protection for their outstretched arms and hands that need to control the horses’ reigns. They wear helmets to protect the back of their necks. More than 8,000 life-size vivid soldier and horse figures in battle formation accompany their emperor into immortality.

The Qin Great Wall

Before the unification of China, battles between states happened frequently to expand territory. Therefore, the princes and overlords began to build high walls to keep intruders out The Qi State was the first state to build such walls in 656 BC. From then on, princes and overlords from other states began to build walls on their borders. After the unification of China, Qin Shi Huang linked the walls of the three northern states - Qin, Zhao, and Yan. The Qin Dynasty Great Wall formed the first “5,000-Kilometer Great Wall”. This Great Wall linked walls in today’s Gansu, Ningxia Hui Autonomous Region, Shaanxi and Shanxi provinces, Inner Mongolia, Hebei, Liaoning and North Korea.

The Epang Palace

The Epang Palace in western Xi’ an, was the imperial palace of Qin Dynasty. As one of the famous constructions in Chinese history, the site of the palace is listed as a Major Historical and Cultural Site Protected at the National LeveL After Qin united the six states, Qin Shi Huang forced over 700,000 people to build the Epang Palace in 212 BC. but only the front hall was completed during his reign. After his death, the succeeding emperor continued the construction, with a storied building built every 5 steps and a pavilion every 10 steps. Legend has it that Xiang Yu set Epang Palace on fire. But archeologists suggest the palace he destroyed was, in fact, Xianyang palace.
