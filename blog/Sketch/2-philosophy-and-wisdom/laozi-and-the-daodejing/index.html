Laozi and the Daodejing

  • Culture
  • Philosophy , Personage

Laozi was a philosopher of ancient China and was a central figure in Taoism, also spelled “Daoism”. Laozi means “Old Master” and is generally considered an honorific Laozi is revered as a god in religious forms of Taoism. With his profound wisdom came a great sense of humor and playfulness - qualities found frequently among the Taoist masters.

Very little is known about the historical life of Laozi. What we do know is that he lived in the 6th century BC, his birth name was Li Erh, and that he was a native of the southern feudal state of Chu. As an adult, he held a minor government post as a librarian in the imperial archives. At some point he relinquished this post-presumably to engage more deeply with his spiritual path.

As legend has it, Laozi underwent a profound spiritual awakening and then traveled to the Hangu Pass, where he disappeared forever, into the land of the Immortals. The last person that he encountered was a gatekeeper, who requested that Laozi offer him and all of humanity the essence of the wisdom that had been revealed to him. In response to this request, Laozi dictated what was to become known as the Daodejing (also spelled Tao Te Ching). Along with the Zhuangzi and the Liezi, the 5,000 words Daodejing forms the textual core of Daojia or philosophical Taoism.

Laozi ’s magnum opus, the Daodejing, is one of the most significant treatises in Chinese cosmogony. As with most other ancient Chinese philosophers, Laozi often explains his ideas by way of paradox, analogy, appropriation of ancient sayings, repetition, symmetry, rhyme and rhythm.

The Daodejing often called simply the Laozi after its reputed author, describes the Dao as the mystical source and ideal of all existence: it is unseen, but not transcendent, immensely powerful yet supremely humble, being the root of all things. The Daodejing is widely considered to be the most influential Taoist text. It is a foundational scripture of central importance in Taoism.

The opening sentences, with a literal translation, are: The Dao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Dao. The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name. (Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth; (Conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things.

Dao means “path” or “way” and can figuratively mean “essential nature” “destiny” “principle” or “true path”. The philosophical and religious “Dao” is the origin of all creation and force, unknowable in its essence but observable in its manifestations, that lie behind the functioning and changes of the natural world. It is infinite without limitation. Dao is believed to be transcendent, indistinct and without form. Hence, it cannot be named or categorized. Even the word “Dao” can be considered a dangerous temptation to make Dao a limiting “name”.

Zhuangzi

Zhuangzi ’s thought is contained in Zhuangzi, which describes both his philosophy and his way of life. In it, Zhuangzi enlarges the teachings of Laozi. Zhuangzi argues that the processes of nature unify all things so that human beings should seek to live in harmony with nature and not impose upon it. He concludes that one could do more by doing nothing. He views nature as having great spontaneity and change, with all things - large or small, beautiful or ugly - equally important and ever in constant flux. In this way, he expands the notion of co-dependence of things, one causing the change in another. Zhuangzi also emphasizes the mutual causation of opposites: for example, that life leads to death. His dislike of formal structures leads him to put forward his ideas in imaginary dialogues.

Wuwei

One of Taoism’s most important concepts is Wuwei, which is sometimes translated as “non-doing” or “non-action”. In the Daodejing, Laozi explains that humans have no special place within the Dao. We are just one of its many manifestations. Many acts “unnaturally”, upsetting the natural balance of the Dao. Humans that are wholly in harmony with the Dao behave in a completely natural, uncontrived way. The Daodejing intends to lead people to a “return” to their natural state. Language and conventional wisdom are critically assessed. Taoism views them as inherently biased and artificial.
