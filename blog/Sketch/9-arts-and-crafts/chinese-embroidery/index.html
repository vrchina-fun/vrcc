Chinese Embroidery

  • Art
  • Craft

The earliest examples of Chinese embroidery stem from the Zhou Dynasty. These might well represent the origin of embroidery, but early examples of which have also been discovered in ancient Egypt and northern Europe, though there was no conclusive proof of exactly where embroidery originated. Ancient Chinese embroidery was crafted using silk since the Chinese had already learned how to spin silk threads from silkworms. Curiously, Chinese embroidery was originally the domain of males; it was only later that Chinese men realized that the delicate hands of their women were better suited to the task.

The art of embroidery had become widespread throughout China by the time of the Han Dynasty. Four distinctive styles, or schools, of embroidery, emerged in China at that time, though each of them would quite naturally reach its respective pinnacle during a much later period, especially after the blooming of Silk Road trade with India and Middle East Europe had created a market for Chinese embroidery, a market where the demand for ever more intricate embroidery in ever richer patterns would be met by Chinese artisans.

The four schools of Chinese embroidery - all of them, collectively and individually, now designated by the Chinese government as a Chinese Intangible Cultural Heritage are as following:

The Shu School of Chinese Embroidery

The art of Shu embroidery follows certain strict, tradition-bound principles, which can be divided into twelve primary weaving categories that in tum result in 122 subcategories. Shu embroidery is typically done on soft satin fabric which, to this today, is still produced. Typical Shu embroidery products include quilt covers and pillowcases, table cloths and chair cushions, and scarves and handkerchiefs.

The Xiang School of Chinese Embroidery

Xiang embroidery items are things of bold beauty. A critic might say that they are garish because they emphasize few but bold colors, with large solid-color surfaces. Delicate understatement and intricacy of the pattern was never the goal, but instead simple and bold, unapologetic beauty that exudes a luxurious plushness. Xiang embroidery was much prized in China during especially the later period of the Qing Dynasty.

The Su School of Chinese Embroidery

Su embroidery became so famous throughout China during the latter part of the Qing Dynasty that the city of Suzhou was given the title of “City of Embroidery”. Su embroidery became the preferred embroidery of the Qing Dynasty court, both for official royal costumes as well as for wall decoration, such as tapestries. It also introduced gilded and silver-coated threads and developed designs with patterns that became very popular, especially at court.

The Yue School of Chinese Embroidery

Yue embroideries are as richly colored like any other school of Chinese embroidery. They are especially popular with Chinese communities all over the world, including the closer-to-home Chinese communities in Hong Kong and Macao. Yue embroidery has also won many prizes, including prizes at international expositions. Some of the older exemplars of Yue embroidery fetch outrageous prices at international auctions such as Christies and Sotheby’s whenever they appear.

Batik of China

Batik is a traditional Chinese folk art that combines painting and dyeing. It is made by dipping a specially designed cloth into the melted wax and painting various patterns on pieces of white cloth. The wax stays on the cloth and often cracks after it hardens. The cloth is then dyed and the dyes seep into the cracks and make fine lines. When the wax is boiled away, beautiful patterns appear on the cloth. Batik cloth can be made into garments, scarves, bags, table-cloths, bedspreads, curtains and other decorative items.

The history of batik can be traced back to the Western Han Dynasty. Batik used to be popular both in Central and Southwest China. Somehow the batik technique was lost in Central China, but it has been handed down from one generation to the next among the ethnic people in Guizhou, a province in Southwest China.

Chinese Knots

Traditional Chinese Knots are a decorative handicraft art that began as a form of Chinese folk art in the Tang and Song Dynasties in China. It was later popularized in the Ming and Qing Dynasties. The art is also referred to as Chinese traditional decorative knots.

In the Chinese language, “knot” has the meanings of reunion, friendliness, warmth, marriage, and love, etc., so Chinese knots are often used to express good wishes including happiness, prosperity, love and good luck. Therefore, this unique artwork of Chinese knots has been a popular gift for years and embodies best wishes.
