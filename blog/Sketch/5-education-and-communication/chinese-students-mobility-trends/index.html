Chinese Students Mobility Trends

  • Society
  • Education , Language

With a population of almost 1.4 billion and a burgeoning middle class, China sends more students abroad than any other country in the world. Around 460,000 Chinese students were enrolled in foreign educational institutions in 2014, a rise of eleven percent over the previous year. The U.S. is a major destination for these students, with fully two-thirds of these students enrolled in the U.S. institutions in the 2014/2015 school year.

Most students enroll in institutions of higher education, while junior and senior high school students account for approximately thirty percent of the total.

Chinese students began to arrive in the U.S. in the late 1970s. Since then, their numbers have increased by up to thirty percent year on year, reaching a high of 304,040 in 2014/2015. The U.S. is now the top host country for students from China; California, New York, and Texas are the leading destinations. Driving factors include fierce competition for admission to China’s elite universities, the prestige of studying at American universities and China’s rapid economic growth. Among Chinese students at American universities now, forty-one percent are undergraduates and thirty-nine percent are graduate students. The most popular fields of study for these students are science, technology, engineering, and mathematics, followed by business studies. A growing number have also begun to enroll in fine and applied for arts programs. Joint Chinese-foreign educational programs and institutions in China are increasingly common. These include partnerships between Chinese and foreign universities, such as New York University with East China Normal University Shanghai and the Joint Institute of Engineering. Increasingly popular “2+2” programs allow students to study for two years at a Chinese university and for two years at a university in another country such as the U.K., Australia or the U.S. Partnerships between Chinese and American universities on American soil, notably the Global Innovation Exchange, are also increasingly common. A joint project of Tsinghua University, the University of Washington and Microsoft will offer graduate degrees in technology-related fields.

China also uses education as a form of “soft power” to increase her influence in other countries. In 2010, the Chinese government announced plans to increase the number of international students in China to five hundred thousand by 2020. To attract foreign academics and students, it has substantially increased the number of undergraduate and graduate programs at Chinese universities taught exclusively in English. Plan 111, launched in 2005, aims to attract global talents to China’s top-tier universities. Since the early 1990s, generous funds have also been made available to encourage Chinese star academics working abroad to return to their home country.

Now we have the Confucius Institute program, which operates at higher education institutions around the globe to promote Chinese language and culture. Another effort, the China Scholarship Council, provides funding for both Chinese students abroad and foreign students in China. A spate r:D of recent measures also seeks to strengthen ties with specific regions. For instance, in early 2016, China’s government announced the establishment of 10,000 scholarships for nationals of Arab League member states and 30, 000 scholarships for students from Africa.

Confucius Institutes

Benefiting from the UK, France, Germany and Spain’s experience in promoting their national languages, China began its own exploration through establishing Confucius Institute’s non-profit public institutions which aim to promote the Chinese language and culture in foreign countries in 2004. At present, China has established many Confucius Institutes along the “Belt and Road”. “Peace is most precious”, “harmony without uniformity” and other concepts in Confucian traditional culture have been understood and accepted by more and more foreign students. The headquarters are located in Beijing. Its functions are to make development plans and set down establishment and evaluation criterion for Confucius Institutes, to guide and evaluate the teaching activities and to control the quality of the operation of Confucius Institutes.

An Introduction to the HSK Test

The Chinese Proficiency Test is China’s national standardized test designed and developed by the HSK Center of Beijing Language and Culture University to assess the Chinese language proficiency of non-native speakers. HSK is divided into three categories: beginning level, elementary to intermediate level and advanced leveL Beginning level is a standardized test designed for beginners. It is most appropriate for those who have acquired one hundred and fifty to three hundred basic Chinese words. HSK Elementary-Intermediate is suitable for people who have mastery of six hundred to one thousand and two hundred Chinese words and certain rules of grammar referring to levels A, B and C in the outline of HSK Grammar. HSK Advanced applies to those who have mastery of two thousand and five hundred to five thousand Chinese words and the corresponding rules of grammar.
