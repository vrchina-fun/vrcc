Chinese Jade Burial Suits

  • Art
  • Craft , Heritage , History

Just fifteen of the extraordinary head-to-toe jade suits that promised immortality to the Chinese imperial family have ever been found. The ancient Chinese believe that when a person dies, he or she will enter into the afterlife. Death is comprehended as a prolongation of life, and an emperor’s mausoleum is his after-life palace, mirroring his regal life on earth. All of the daily comforts of their past life such as servants, attendants, objects, pets, wives, guardians, concubines, food and drink should be provided for them in the afterlife. This is accomplished by burying all of these things with the deceased when they die. As an ancient Chinese philosopher said: “Treat death as life.”, so it was not uncommon to kill people to be buried with their master, but as dynasties evolved clay replicas replaced the real thing.

Corpses, such as that of emperor Liu Sheng and his wife the princess Dou Wan, have been found in spectacular jade suits made of thousands of small plaques sewn together with gold thread. Liu Sheng’s tomb in Mancheng, Hebei was built like an actual house with horses, stables, windows, storerooms with cookbooks and even a bathroom. Most of the 2,800 items found in this tomb are unique. Besides the jade suits and other jade products, the famous Changxin palace lantern and a gold-inlaid furnace are also included.

The making of a jade burial suit was not an easy job, because there were strict requirements on techniques. First, jade materials transported from far-away places were processed into thousands of small jade pieces of certain shapes and sizes after a great many procedures; second, each jade piece was polished and drilled, with the shapes and sizes of the holes undergoing special scrutiny and delicate processing; third, a lot of specially made gold, silver or copper threads were used to join the jade pieces together. The finished suits were respectively called “gold thread sewn jade burial suit”, “silver thread is sewn jade burial suit” and “copper thread is sewn jade burial suit”. The shape of the suit was the same as the human body. The cost of making a medium-sized jade burial suit was almost equal to the property values of 100 then middle-class families put together. You can have an idea about what an extravagance the suit was.

Jade burial objects were used as early as the Neolithic Age in China. The custom of using jade suits prevailed during the Han Dynasty, based on the belief that it would keep the corpse from decomposing. According to history books, jade suits were made in accordance with rigid rules. Emperors were buried in jade suits sewn with gold thread. The silver thread was used for princes and princesses, copper thread for concubines and sisters of the emperors and silk thread for high-ranking aristocrats. Ordinary officials and commoners were not allowed to use jade suits.

The custom of using jade burial suits continued for about 400 years until the end of the Eastern Han Dynasty. In the following period, the Three Kingdoms, Cao Pi, Emperor Wen Di of the Wei Kingdom, gave orders forbidding the use of jade suits for burials because he found that all the imperial tombs of the Han Dynasty, without exception, had been robbed, their valuable contents looted and the corpses left exposed.

Chinese Bianzhong Bells

In 1978, a huge set of bronze bells was discovered during an excavation in China. This rare set of instruments was found in decent condition in the tomb of Marquis Yi of Zeng and is fully functional even after so many years. These more than 2000-year-old bronze bells look like truncated cones. Known as Bianzhong, the complete set constitutes an ancient Chinese musical instrument. The 65 bells with 130 different strike tones are considered one of the world’s most important archaeological discoveries. Hung in a wooden frame and played melodically, by striking them with a mallet, the bells produce magnificent music which is very rare in the world’s cultural history. The bells have an amazing two-tone quality, each bell giving two different tones depending on where it is struck.

Houmuwu Square Cauldron

The Houmuwu Square Cauldron is one of the common types of Chinese ritual bronzes of the ancient Chinese Shang dynasty. It is the largest piece of bronze ware to survive from anywhere in the ancient world. It was unearthed in Wuguan Village, Anyang, Henan province in 1939. It is a rectangular vessel with a protruding rim and thick square lip. Each side has a blank space in the middle, surrounded by a band of decoration featuring animal faced creatures and one-legged dragons, which are called Kuilong. There are two handles, each decorated on the exterior with two tigers facing each other. Three characters are inscribed on the inside of the body: “Hou Mu Wu”, written in a vigorous and robust script, with full shape and strokes which taper into points at their ends but are full in the middle.
