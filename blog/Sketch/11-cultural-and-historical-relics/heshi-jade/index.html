Heshi Jade

  • Art
  • Craft , Heritage , History

From Neolithic times, the Chinese have been extremely fond of jade and have made this magical stone an inseparable part of Chinese culture and art.

In China the national passion for jade gave that stone much the same superiority that lapis lazuli had in Sumer and the Chinese saying “Gold has a value; jade is invaluable.” also proved its position among Chinese people.

After 30 years of geological study in Jingshan Mountain and its surrounding areas, in 2014, Chinese researchers finally unearthed a rarely high-quality gem with ultra-hardness, Jinyu and seven other types of jade objects in Baokang, Hubei province.

Jinyu is classified as a new type of jade. Its hardness is generally between 8 and 10 degrees. Jinyu contains precious metals and trace elements. The hardness, gloss, and color of Jinyu match the historical records of the jade disc in the legendary story of Heshi Bi which dates back 2,000 years ago.

Heshi Bi, literally means “Jade Disc of He”, is a piece of jade, which is perhaps the most valuable piece of jade in Chinese recorded history. It was first made into a jade disc from a single piece of “Hetian nephrite, discovered in the ancient State of the Chu in Eastern China.

According to an ancient legend, this perfect specimen of jade was concealed in an outer stone layer rather than carved into the precious stone. Bian He, a very experienced jade craftsman, presented it to the emperor Li of the Chu State. Emperor Li did not believe the value of the jade that lay within and cut off one of Bian He’s feet to punish him for not being honest to him. Later emperor Li died and he was succeeded by emperor Wu. Bian He presented this stone to the new emperor again but got the same result and another foot was cut off Until emperor Wen took the throne and heard Bian He’s story, he had his best sculptors cut into the rough stone where they found an incomparable piece of white jade. The jade was cut into a disc, and named in honor of Bian He, becoming a national treasure known as the “Heshi Bi”.

The jade disc was stolen from the Chu and eventually sold to Zhao; in 283 BC, King Zhaoxiang of Qin offered 15 cities to the State of Zhao in exchange for the jade. Zhao Minister Lin Xiangru was dispatched to send the jade to Qin. When it became clear that Qin would not uphold its side of the bargain, he tricked the king of Qin, claiming that the jade had a small defect on it The marquess of Qin said he could not find it, and asked Lin Xiangru to show him where the scar was. The moment Lin Xiangru took the jade, he threatened to smash it unless the king of Qin promised to delay the swap by 3 days. Then he told his servants to secretly take the jade back to the king of the Zhao. In 221 BC, Qin conquered the other six states and the jade finally came into Qin’s possession. Under the order of Qin Shi Huang, the first emperor of China, it was made into his Imperial Seal and had the seal inscribed with the words ‘‘The Mandate is received from Heaven. May He have Longevity and Prosperity”.

Jade Dragon

This is the earliest jade dragon discovered in China and dates back 5,000 years ago. With both material and spiritual force, it is a rare work of art, and has become famous as “The First Dragon of China”. The dragon has protruding tear-shaped oval eyes, an upturned nose with a flat end and two nostrils, showing the typical characteristic of a pig’s snout. The mouth is depicted using fine lines. There is a regular network of fine lines on its forehead and jaw. Its mane extends along the back of its neck and spine and curls upwards like a long hook.

Tomb Figurine of a Storyteller

This piece of artwork was discovered in the central part of the corridor of the sandstone tomb. It was made in reddish clay from a combination of casting and hand molding. The figurine wears a kerchief coiled around his head and knotted at the front. His torso is naked showing his drooping belly and he wears bracelets on both arms, the one on the left made of pearls. His left arm is curled around a circular drum, 11.5cm in diameter. The 19cm-long drumstick is in his raised right hand as if he is about to beat the drum. He raises his head, his eyes crinkled and his forehead creased with laughter. This is a joyous piece, filled with humor and a spirit, bringing to life a performing artist of 2,000 years ago.
