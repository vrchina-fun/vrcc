Crested Ibis

  • Species
  • Nature , Animal

The crested ibis is a rare and precious species in the world. In history, crested ibises were widely distributed in East Asia. Since the middle of the 20th century, its population has reduced rapidly due to the change of the inadaptable environment caused by human activities, such as the decrease of the rice fields, the wide applications of fertilizers and pesticides and the reduction of forest area. The sharp reduction of the number of crested ibises makes it a kind of rarer animal.

The body of a crested ibis is about 79 centimeters long and 1.8 kilograms in weight The male and female ones have similar white plumages with a slight tinge of pink at the root of the feather. Its forehead and face are bare, showing red skin and it has a dense, willow leaf-like crest on the nape. When it flies, vermeil from behind the wings and tail come out from the pure white plumage, looking graceful and beautifuL Due to its gentle nature, the crested ibis is called lucky bird and is considered as the symbol of good luck and happiness by Chinese people.

The crested ibises inhabit temperate forests and hills, neighboring with wetlands such as rice fields, riverbanks, streams, ponds, and marshes. Being solitary, quiet and shy, they usually fly and forage in pairs or small groups. The crested ibises are quite sensitive to the environment and they only rest or nest on tall trees where there are rice fields and marshes nearby for foraging with a quiet environment and few natural enemies.

In China, the crested ibises used to be widely distributed in Heilongjiang, Jilin, Liaoning, Hebei, Beijing, Shanxi, Shaanxi, Gansu, Inner Mongolia, Henan, Shandong, Anhui, Jiangsu, Jiangxi, Shanghai, Zhejiang, Fujian, and Taiwan. At the beginning of the 20th century, the population of the crested ibises declined sharply and it was on the verge of extinction in 1978. Meanwhile, the last wild crested ibis in Japan died and the other six captive ones were not able to reproduce anymore. However, in May of 1981, a small group of seven wild crested ibises was discovered in Yang County, Hanzhong City of Shaanxi Province, is the only population of wild crested ibis in the world. Since then, great efforts have been made on the protection and scientific research of crested ibises and noticeable achievements have been made in these fields, especially in captive breeding. In 1989, the world’s first captive-bred bird was born, and since 1992, the captive-bred fledging could manage to survive. By 1995, there were about 35 crested ibises in the wild and 25 ones in captivity, making it possible to save this rare species from extinction. At present, the total population of crested ibises in China has raised to about 2000.

Black-necked Crane

The black-necked crane is endemic to the Tibetan Plateau. In summer, the black-necked crane is found at extremely high elevations, typically 3,000-4,900 meters, on the central and northern Tibetan Plateau where it nests in high altitude wetlands throughout Qinghai and the northern Tibet Autonomous Region as well as in adjacent areas of Xinjiang, Gansu, western Sichuan, and the Ladakh region of India. In winter, the black-necked crane migrates south, with about two-thirds of an estimated total population of 11,000 wintering on croplands in the valleys of the Yarlung Tsangpo River and its major tributaries in the south-central Tibet Autonomous Region.

Chinese Sturgeon

Sturgeons are comparatively basal species of fish, whose earliest fossils date back to the Cretaceous period. They are best-known members of the bony fish taxon Chondrostei, a group of bony fishes that have cartilaginous skeletons superficially similar to the skeletons seen in the unrelated chondrichthyan fishes.

An adult Chinese sturgeon can range between two to five meters in total length, and weigh between 200 to 500 kg, ranking them among the largest sturgeons in the world. Its head is acuminate, with the mouth under its jaw. Most sturgeons spawn in freshwater and migrate to saltwater to mature. A Chinese sturgeon can be considered a large freshwater fish, although it spends part of its life-cycle in seawater, like the salmon, except a Chinese sturgeon spawns multiple times throughout their lives.
