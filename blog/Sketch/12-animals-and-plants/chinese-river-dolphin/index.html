Chinese River Dolphin

  • Species
  • Nature , Animal

The Chinese river dolphin is a freshwater dolphin and one of the most endangered animals on Earth. It is also known as the Yangtze River dolphin, baiji, white-flag dolphin, and white-fin dolphin. Nicknamed “Goddess of the Yangtze”, it was regarded as the goddess of protection by local fishermen and boatmen in China.

The Yangtze River dolphin is pale blue to gray on the dorsal side, white on the ventral side. It has a long and slightly-upturned beak with 31-36 conical teeth on either jaw. Its dorsal fin is low and triangular in shape and resembles a light-colored flag when the dolphin swims just below the surface of the murky Yangtze River, hence the name “white-flag” dolphin. It has smaller eyes compared to oceanic dolphins. A mature Yangtze River dolphin is about eight feet long and weighs about 500 pounds.

Just like the other species of river dolphins, the Yangtze River dolphin originated from the ocean. The ancestor of the Yangtze River dolphin resided in the shallow sea that inundated the Yangtze River Basin during the globally high sea levels of the Middle Miocene period (approximately 20 million years ago). After the subsequent sea-level lowering and transition to a freshwater river during the late Neogene period, the dolphins remained and evolved to become the present-day Yangtze River dolphins.

The Yangtze River dolphin is thought to breed in the first half of the year, with the peak calving season between February and April. Gestation lasts ten to eleven months and a newborn calf measures about three feet. Dolphins reach sexual maturity at four to six years of age. The life expectancy for the Yangtze River dolphin is about twenty-five years, based on an individual in captivity.

Very little is known about the ecology, behavior, and acoustics of the Yangtze River dolphin. Groups of two to six animals are most commonly seen, although aggregations of up to sixteen animals have also been observed. The Yangtze River dolphin feeds on a large variety of freshwater fish species. These dolphins are generally shy of boats, and often expose only the top of the head, dorsal fin and a small part of the back when surfacing.

Due to the poor visibility in the murky Yangtze River, the Yangtze River dolphin depends largely on underwater sound for orientation, feeding, and communication. Just like most dolphin species, the Yangtze River dolphin produces two types of sounds: clicks and whistles. Clicks are believed to be used primarily for navigation and identifying prey and other objects in the surrounding environment. Whistles are frequency modulated (pitch changes with time) sounds and are thought to be used for communication.

Tibetan Antelope

The Tibetan antelope is endemic to the Tibetan Plateau. It weighs 26-40 kilograms and favors alpine steppe or similar semiarid habitats. The Tibetan antelope prefers flat to rolling terrain, although it readily ascends high rounded hills and penetrates mountains and crosses pass by following valleys. It can be found at elevations from 3,250 - 5,500 meters. Tibetan antelope herds also vary widely in size and composition. The Tibetan antelope is found primarily on the alpine steppe of northwest Tibet in China. There are a number of both migratory as well as resident populations.

Pere David’s Deer

Pere David’s deer, or Milu in Chinese, is native to the subtropics of China. This mammal has almost been rescued from the brink of extinction, owing to the widespread effort of Father Armand David, who was running a French missionary in China. The breed has, thus, derived its name from its saver, Father (‘Pere’ in French) David and is known as Pere David’s deer worldwide. This species of deer is characterized by branched antlers, heavy legs, large eyes, broad hooves, relatively small ears, and a long, bushy tail. The animal loves water and is a great swimmer.
