The Tomb Sweeping Day

  • Folklore
  • Festival , History

The Tomb Sweeping Day also called “Qing Ming” in Chinese has been a traditional Chinese practice for over 2,500 years and falls on the 15th day of the Spring Equinox, which usually falls on the 4th or 5th of April. In the most traditional sense, families gather together to worship their ancestors and provide maintenance such as cleaning, sweeping, repairing tombstones and placing flowers.

During the day there are different practices that people follow. To ward off evil spirits, some families plant willow trees or wear a willow tree branch as the line between the afterlife and the living world is thin.

The great benefit of celebrating the Tomb Sweeping Day is the chance to reflect on ancestors’ lives. Young children can rely on intricate stories told by their elders who offer a personal connection to relatives they never had the chance to meet. Among young people in their twenties, you may hear, “I feel as if they’re watching over and protecting us when we go to sweep their graves and share stories”. A contemporary and familiar image would be Disney’s Mulan literally speaking to her ancestors’ spirits as they give her advice and encouragement.

Another name for this day is “Cold Food Day”. Eating cold food means eating raw food, which was originated in the Spring and Autumn Period over 2,000 years ago. Jiezitui once saved Prince Chong’er’s life, so when Chong’er resumed the throne, he wanted to offer Jiezitui a position, but Jiezitui declined and brought his mother to hide in the mountains. Chong’er could do nothing but bum the mountains in the hope of forcing them to come out. Unfortunately the big fire burned them to death, so in memory of Jiezitui, the emperor (Jin Wen-gong) decreed that fire was forbidden on that day every year, and the households could only eat raw and cold food.

The Tomb Sweeping Day is one of the 24 solar terms in China, indicating the coming of late spring, thus the best plowing and growing time, while “Cold Food Day” is a day when folks sweep the ancestors’ tombs and eat cold food. Tomb Sweeping Day was adjacent to Cold Food Day, so, later on, they gradually became one festival, and thus “Cold Food” became another name for the Tomb Sweeping Day, and dusting the tomb and eating cold food turned into the customs of the Tomb Sweeping Day. This festival has evolved into a culture-rich and meaning-deep remembrance day.

Although the Tomb Sweeping Day may be a solemn time for some, this period is also called the ‘Tread Green’, when families also have picnics by the graves or in nearby greenery, and fly kites or view the spring blossoms. The reverence of life and death neatly spread across a three day period of rest, reflection, and respect for family and nature truly encapsulates the Chinese philosophy of"Yin and Yang” where opposites are complementary and create true balance in life.

Kite-flying

The custom of kite-flying prevails during the Tomb Sweeping Day. The bamboo crossbow of the kite vibrates with a buzz as it rises aloft on the power of its tail.

Big kites can be as broad as about three square meters with a tail of six to ten meters. Most kites have a rectangular shape. The rest are patterned as crabs, centipedes, butterflies, and dragonflies, or Chinese characters such as “fortune” or “longevity”. All kites are constructed with fine craftsmanship. When flown in the evening, they have lights attached to their tails and some have as many as three to five lights strung together.

Planting Trees

Around the Tomb Sweeping Day, the sun of spring shines brightly, and the rain of spring drops sufficiently, which helps saplings to survive and grow. The custom of planting trees on the Tomb Sweeping Day can be dated back to ancient times in China. Someone even regards the period of Tomb Sweeping Day as the “Arbor Day”. And this custom is still popular nowadays. In 1979, the Standing Committee of National People’s Congress provided March 12th of every year as the Arbor Day of China. This makes significant sense in mobilizing people of all nationalities to participate in activities to afforest China.
