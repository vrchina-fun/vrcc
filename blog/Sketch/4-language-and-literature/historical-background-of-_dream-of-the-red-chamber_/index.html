Historical Background of Dream of the Red Chamber

  • Art
  • Literature , Personage , Story

This massive, sprawling novel of China was written in the mid-eighteenth century. Cao Xueqin, the author of “Dream of the Red Chamber”, was born and raised in an aristocratic family, but he died in misery and isolation. From his own bitter and personal experiences, Cao created a tragic love story between a young man, Jia Baoyu, and a young woman, Lin Daiyu. Along with their love story, he described in careful detail the ups and downs of four leading aristocratic families: Jia, Shi, Wang, and Xue.

It was during this period that a vast amount of land was annexed and concentrated in the hands of the royal families, the aristocrats, the bureaucratic landlords, and big businessmen, while the peasants who lost their lands were destined to become the landlords’ tenants. This social division became a terrible abyss. Even small landlords were often on the brink of bankruptcy. This critical social and economic situation could be seen in the very first chapter of “Dream of the Red Chamber”: after a devastating fire, a small landlord, Zhen Shiyin, went bankrupt and must seek refuge with his father-in-law.

Many monopoly groups emerged during this period, exploiting the peasants politically and economically. The author’s description of the four major families of Jia, Shi, Wang, and Xue, Jia family being representative of the group as a whole, was deeply rooted in the social reality of the time. Racketeering and extortion fJ) flourished in this era. There were corrupt officials at every level, and the usury and heavy levies from the landlords were so unfair and unbearable that the peasants could do nothing but take a fearful gamble. They were forced to revolt.

The people ’s dissatisfaction with the political corruption of their country can also be found in the historical records, particularly those describing the textile workers’ strikes in Suzhou and Nanjing, where Cao Xueqin spent his childhood after rising capitalism gained its prominent position in the country’s economy.

At the same time, within the elite ruling class of the Qing Dynasty, contradictions and conflicts between different political forces and exploitation cliques became increasingly sharp and tense. In the late years of Kangxi’s reign, an intense struggle for the seizure of the throne was fought among China’s top ruling leaders. Kangxi’s fourth son, a very clever, sophisticated person, fiercely wanted to gain his father ’s throne and went out of his way to make friends with people of all ranks and classes. He was finally successful, and, once in power, he adopted every possible measure to rid the court of all his political enemies- including his father’s followers and his own brothers.

The emperor Qian Long followed Yong Zheng’s example. Once he came to the throne, he did the same thing. These frightening conditions created a terrifying atmosphere in the court and throughout the country, as well. At this time, Cao Xueqin’s family was not popular with the royal families; therefore, it was clearly on the road to social and financial decline.

“Moment in Peking”

“Moment in Peking” is a historical novel set in twentieth-century China. Written in English by the Chinese American author Lin Yutang, it covers the turbulent events from the Boxer Uprising in 1900 to the Anti-Japanese War of 1938. The author tried not to be overly judgmental of his characters. There are no absolutely good or bad characters, each holding a piece of truth as well as illusion. When the novel was published in the United States at the end of 1939, it sold 50,000 copies in just six months. TIME magazine said “it may well become the classic background novel of modem China.”

Mo Yan

Chinese author Mo Yan has become the first Chinese citizen ever to win the Nobel Prize in literature, praised by the Swedish Academy for merging “folk tales, history and the contemporary” with “hallucinatory realism”. The win in 2012 made Mo Yan the first Chinese citizen to win the Nobel in its one hundred and eleven years of history. Born in 1955 to parents who were farmers, Mo Yan- a pseudonym for Guan Moye meaning “don’t speak” - grew up in Gaomi in Shandong province in north-eastern China. He published his first book in 1981 but found literary success in 1987 with Red Sorghum Clan, which set against the horrific events unfolded as Japan invaded China in the 1930s.
