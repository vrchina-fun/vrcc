The Silk Road Dream of China's High-speed Trains

  • Society
  • Technology , Heritage , History

Generally speaking, a rail network that offers 200-350 km/h trains is an HSR (High-Speed Railway) network in China. High-speed trains were once the preserve of Japan, with its “bullet train”, and France, with its TG~. But now China’s trains are the world’s fastest, the network the longest and its expansion plans the most ambitious. At the end of 2016, there were more than 20,000 kilometers of high-speed rail lines in service.

China is a relatively new emergence in the high-speed train field. At the very early stage, due to historical reasons, China’s overall railway technology and equipment are similar to that of developed countries’ rail systems in the 1970s; high-speed rolling stock development is still in its infancy stage. If using only our resources and expertise, China might need a decade or longer to catch up with developed nations. In 2004, the Chinese State Council and the Ministry of Railways defined a modem railway technology and equipment policy as “the introduction of advanced technology, the joint design, and production, to build China brand”. The realization of the railway “leapfrog development” is the key task required for high-speed trains. On 9 April 2004, the Chinese government held a conference on modem railway equipment and rolling stock, in which they drafted the current Chinese plan to modernize China’s railway infrastructure with advanced technologies. By 2012, China had had more high-speed train tracks than the rest of the world combined.

With the technological and engineering capabilities advancing rapidly, Chinese railway enterprises have found that projects in developing nations can no longer satisfy their growth ambitions, so China has begun to knock on the doors of some developed countries that once looked down on Chinese railway technology.

In 2014, China’s first overseas high-speed rail project was completed in Turkey where a 533-km railway links capital Ankara with Istanbul. In June 2015, a consortium of Chinese and Russian companies agrees on a contract worth 20.8 billion rubles with Russian Railways on pre-construction surveys and design for 770 km of track linking Moscow and Kazan. It is the first time Chinese railway technology is used abroad. In October 2015, China and Indonesia signed a joint-venture agreement on construction and operation of a high-speed link between Jakarta and Bandung and according to the news at the beginning of 2017, this project is under construction. Though not all overseas high-speed railway projects go smoothly, these achievements revolutionize the flow of people and goods in the field of the world’s transportation system.

In China, some of the projects are in harsh environments such as the high-altitude Qinghai-Tibet Plateau and bitterly cold Northeast China, testifying to the country’s strong capability in this field. They facilitate the flow of resources in different regions and play an important role in narrowing the gap between eastern and western China.

Wheelbarrow -A Practical Vehicle

The legend goes that it is Zhuge Liang who has invented the vehicle with the single wheel, which is called a “wooden ox”. Using the “wooden ox”, a single soldier could easily carry enough food to feed four men for the entire month. This legend is very tidy and satisfying, but probably untrue. Archaeological evidence suggests that Chinese people were using the wheelbarrow more than a century before Zhuge Liang’s supposed invention of the device in 231 AD. For example, a wall painting in a tomb near Chengdu, in Sichuan Province, shows a man using a wheelbarrow. And that painting was made in the year 118 AD.

The Beijing-Hangzhou Grand Canal

The Beijing-Hangzhou Grand Canal, one of the World Cultural Heritage sites, is the earliest and the longest artificial river in the world. The Canal is ten times the length of the Suez Canal and twenty-two times that of the Panama Canal. It is 1, 794 kilometers long with a history of over 2,500 years and it starts at Beijing in the north and ends at Hangzhou in the south. Six provinces and five river systems are connected by the canal. The Grand Canal played an important role in Chinese history, transporting foods and goods from south to north China and greatly promoted the administration and defense of China as a union, strengthening economic and cultural exchange between the north and the south.
