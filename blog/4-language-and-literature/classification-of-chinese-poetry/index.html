Classification of Chinese Poetry

  • Art
  • History , Literature , Personage , Language

The development of Chinese poetry can be divided into three main periods: the early period, characterized by folk songs in simple, repetitive forms; the classical period from the Han Dynasty to the fall of the Qing Dynasty, in which several different forms were developed; and the modem period of westernized free verse.

Early Poetry

The Shijing was the first major collection of Chinese poems, collecting both aristocratic poems and more rustic poetry, probably derived from folksongs. A second, the more lyrical and romantic anthology was the Chuci, made up primarily of poems ascribed to the semi-legendary Qu Yuan and his follower Song Yu.

Classical Poetry

During the Han Dynasty, the Chuci evolved into Fu, a type of poem usually in rhymed verse except for introductory and concluding passages that are in prose. Fu is often in the form of questions and answers, often called a poetical essay. One of the fine examples of Fu is Ji Kang’s “Qinfu” or “Poetical Essay in Praise of the Qin”. From the Han Dynasty onwards, Yuefu poems began to develop in a similar process as Shi Jing. Again, these were song lyrics, including original folk songs, court imitations, and versions by known poets. From the second century AD, Yuefu began to develop into Shi or classical poetry, a form that dominated Chinese poetry until the modern era. These poems have five-character or seven-character lines, with a caesura before the last three characters of each line. They are divided into the original Gus hi and Jintishi, a stricter form developed in the Tang Dynasty with rules governing tone patterns and the structure of the content. The greatest writers of Gutishi and Jintishi are often held to be Li Bai and Du Fu respectively.

Towards the end of the Tang Dynasty, the Ci lyric became more popular. Most closely associated with the Song Dynasty, Ci mainly expressed feelings of desire, but the greatest exponents of the form used to address a wide range of topics.

As the Ci gradually became more literary and artificial, the Sanqu, a freer form, based on new popular songs, developed. The use of Sanqu songs in drama marked an important step in the development of vernacular literature.

Modern Poetry

Modem Chinese poems usually do not follow any prescribed pattern. Poetry was revolutionized after the May Fourth Movement when writers tried to use vernacular styles closer to what was being spoken rather than previously prescribed forms. Early twentieth-century poets like Xu Zhimo, Guo Moruo and Wen Yiduo sought to break Chinese poetry from past conventions by adopting western models. For example, Xu consciously followed the style of the Romantic poets with end-rhymes.

Poets like Ai Qing used more liberal running lines and direct diction, which were vastly popular and widely imitated in the contemporary poetic scene. The most important and influential poets are the group known as Misty Poets, who use allusion and hermetic references.

The Classification of Ci

Ci poems in the Song Dynasty indicate another type of poetry at that time. It came into being in the Tang Dynasty and reached its summit in the Song literature. Ci, depending upon its length, can be divided into Xiaoling, small-sized, less than fifty-five characters, Zhong diao, middle-sized, fifty-nine to ninety characters, and Changdiao, long-sized, more than ninety-one characters. Each piece of Ci poem has a title as well as a Cipai which is the name of the tonal pattern and decides the rhythm and form of a verse. The names of Cipai, such as “The Beautiful Lady Yu”(虞美人), “Buddha Dance” (菩萨蛮), and “Wine Spring”(酒泉子), are derived from historical figures or events, discourse and former musical names, although later Ci poems evolved separately from the music.

A Cultural Celebrity - Su Shi

Su Shi was a rare master in Chinese literature history, and one of the most popular figures in history. He was often at odds with a political faction headed by Wang Anshi, and eventually Su was exiled twice to remote places. In Huangzhou, he lived at a farm called Dongpo, from which he took his literary name. He died in Changzhou, Jiangsu province. In character, he was very broad-minded and generous. His poems and Ci all showed his great talent in art and expression. In Chinese history, Su Dongpo was a literary landmark and a symbol of Chinese literature and culture in ancient times. His works represent the top level of literary creation.
