Origin of Chinese Characters

  • Culture
  • Language , History

As one of the most ancient characters, Chinese characters have played a significant role in the development of Chinese culture. Originally, ancient Sumerian and ancient Egyptian symbols existed, but only Chinese characters remain today.

Chinese characters can be dated back to approximately five thousand years ago. Nearly one-fourth of the world’s population uses Chinese characters today. As an art form, Chinese calligraphy remains an integral aspect of Chinese culture. There are 47,035 Chinese characters in the Kangxi Dictionary, the standard national dictionary, which developed during the 18th and 19th centuries, but the precise quantity of Chinese characters is still unknown; numerous, rare variants have accumulated throughout history. Studies from China have shown that 90% of Chinese newspapers and magazines tend to use 3,500 basic characters.

Based on pictographs, Chinese characters combine shapes with sounds and connotations to form unique, block-shaped characters that carry meaning. Archaeological researchers discovered many such signs carved on earthenware excavated from Banpo Village in Xi’an city and Jiangzhai village in Lintong city. The etchings were carved during the Yangshao Culture Period some six thousand years ago. More than four thousand years ago, people living in the Tai’an area of Shandong Province also carved signs on earthenware. Take the character"旦”, meaning dawn, for example, this character symbolizes that the sun rises upwards, crossing the mountains and passing through cloud layers to tell people a new day has begun. It is safe to say that the earthenware signs are the first Chinese characters, which originate from drawings.

The most sophisticated Chinese characters are the inscriptions on tortoiseshell and animal bones called Jiaguwen (甲骨文) of the Shang Dynasty that resemble drawings. To date, China has unearthed 150,000 pieces of animal bones and tortoiseshell, including more than 4,600 distinctive Chinese characters, among which more than 1,700 have been identified. The inscriptions on bones and shells consist of phrases and simple sentences, providing much insight into the Shang Dynasty.

In the Shang Dynasty and Western Zhou Dynasty, there were also inscriptions on bronzeware called “Zhongdingwen” (钟鼎文), which also resembled drawings. After the first emperor of the Qin Dynasty unified China, he unified Chinese characters and introduced “lesser seal” (小篆), - a very beautiful style of characters. Since the lesser seal was very time-consuming, people of the Qin Dynasty further simplified the characters and created a new style, “clerkly script” (隶书). In the Han Dynasty, clerkly script - including another type of calligraphy, “cursive script” (草书), followed by “running script” (行书) - became the main typeface. The official script broke away from the pictographic element of ancient Chinese characters, laying the foundations for “standard script” (楷书). Standard script is standard calligraphy that has been used for the longest period of time, even till today.

Oracle Bone Script

Oracle bone script was the form of Chinese characters used on oracle bones - animal bones or turtle plastrons used in pyromantic divination, in the late 2nd millennium BC, and was the earliest known form of Chinese writing and also the earliest writing in East Asia. The majority of oracle bones were found at the Yinxu site, in today’s Anyang, Henan Province. They recorded pyromantic divinations of the last nine kings of the Shang Dynasty. After the Shang Dynasty was overthrown by the Zhou Dynasty in 1 046 BC, divining with milfoil became more common. The late Shang oracle bone writings, along with a few contemporary characters in a different style cast in bronzes, constitute the earliest significant corpus of Chinese writing.

Radicals of Chinese Characters

A radical is a graphical component of a Chinese character under which the character is traditionally listed in a Chinese dictionary. This component is often a semantic indicator, though in some cases the original semantic connection has become obscure, owing to changes in character meaning over time. In other cases, the radical may be a phonetic component or even an artificially extracted portion of the character. Radicals may appear in any position in a character. For example, the radical “女.” appears on the left side in the characters"姐”, “她”, “好” and “姓”, but it appears at the bottom in “妾”. However, there are regularities in the positioning of radicals within most characters, depending on their function: semantic components tend to appear on the top or on the left side of the character; similarly, phonetic components tend to appear on the right side of the character or at its bottom. These are only loose rules, though, and exceptions exist.
